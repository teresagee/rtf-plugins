# Profile structures — the researched shapes

Distilled from the systems that built the genre — Justin Welsh's profile-as-landing-page method is the anchor (his frameworks remain the foundation even though he's personally moved on from the "LinkedIn growth" lane) — updated against mid-2026 platform reality. Use these as structure, never as wording to copy.

---

## The governing principle

**A profile is a landing page with one job.** Decide the single action a visitor should take, then read the profile top-down the way a stranger does: banner → headline → About's first lines → Featured. Every element either moves the visitor toward the one action or it's noise.

**The 2026 addition — the lane.** LinkedIn's ranking model (the 360Brew-era system; rollout specifics are aggregator-reported, treat timing cautiously, but the direction is confirmed by LinkedIn's own stated signals: relevance, expertise, meaningful engagement) matches what you post against what your profile says you are. A profile that names one clear lane makes every future post rank better. A scattered profile structurally underperforms. The headline and About must name the same lane the operator's posts will live in.

---

## Headline

**Rule: value proposition, not job title.** "Founder & CEO" tells a visitor nothing. The headline answers: who do you help, what do they get, and why you specifically.

Working shapes (fill from `business.md` / `ICP.md` — specificity is the whole game):

- `I help <ICP> <get outcome> — <the proof or mechanism only this operator has>`
- `<What you do, plainly> for <ICP>. <One receipt or differentiator>.`
- `<Lane keyword phrase> | <outcome promise> | <one concrete proof point>`

Checks:

- The lane keyword appears naturally (people search; the algorithm matches semantically). Once. Not a keyword pile separated by pipes for its own sake.
- Proof points must be real and dated-true (from the profiles or the operator). No "helped 1,000+ founders" unless it's literally true.
- Read it as a stranger: would you know within 3 seconds whether this person is for you?

## About section

**Structure: PAS told as a story** (Pain → Agitate → Solution) — Welsh's About formula, and the same skeleton Matt Barker runs on most posts. Written to **one reader**: the ICP, in their own language (pull frustration phrasing from `ICP.md`).

1. **Pain (first 1–3 lines — the truncation budget):** name the reader's problem so precisely they feel seen. These lines show before "see more"; they decide whether anyone clicks. No throat-clearing, no "I'm passionate about."
2. **Agitate:** what the problem costs them — time, money, the version of their business they're not running.
3. **Solution:** the operator enters as the answer — origin story compressed (from `user.md`), the mechanism, and real receipts. First person, specific numbers, named outcomes. This is also the practical defense against AI-pattern suppression: nothing generic survives here.
4. **The one action, stated plainly:** what to do and exactly how — "Join <newsletter> here: <link>" / "Start with the free <asset> in my Featured section."

Craft notes: short paragraphs (4 lines max), white space, plain words, lane keywords woven in naturally for search — never stuffed. Length: long enough to carry the story, short enough that every paragraph earns its place.

## Featured section

**Exactly two items** (Welsh's rule — more items diffuse the click):

1. **Free** — the email-capture asset (lead magnet, newsletter signup) for visitors who aren't ready to buy.
2. **Paid** — the offer, for visitors who are.

Mechanics:

- **Skip the item descriptions.** Without a description there's no "view more" fold — the card is one clean click. Less friction, by design.
- **On-brand images on both cards** — the title and image do all the work. Give the operator an image concept per card.
- Both items point at pages the operator actually controls. If the free asset doesn't exist yet, say so plainly and flag it as the missing piece of the funnel — don't link something weak just to fill the slot.

## Banner

The banner is the first thing a visitor sees and it reinforces the **same one action** — not a second message. Deliver a concept brief:

- **Headline words** (5–8 words, the value proposition compressed)
- **Sub-line** (optional — the proof point or the lane)
- **Visual direction** (colors/asset style from `workspace/assets/` or `tools.md` if brand assets exist; otherwise simple and high-contrast)
- **CTA pointer** (where the eye goes next — typically toward the Featured section or the named free asset)

The skill writes the brief; the operator or their tooling makes the image.

## The rest of the profile (quick passes, not the main event)

- **Photo:** a clear, current face. Personal-profile data through 2025 (Richard van der Blom's algorithm report, 1.8M+ posts) shows real-face images strongly outperform faceless ones in feed — the same logic applies to the profile photo: people follow faces.
- **Experience:** the top entry should describe the current operation in lane terms (what you do for whom), not internal job duties. It's proof-of-lane, not a resume.
- **Settings that replaced Creator Mode** (retired March 2024): follow-primary on, newsletter feature if they publish one, profile analytics. Any guide that says "turn on Creator Mode" is pre-2024 and stale.

## Dated practices — do not ship these

- **Creator Mode / "talks about" hashtags** — feature retired March 2024; the hashtag-topics line no longer exists.
- **Hashtag optimization anywhere** — hashtag pages were disabled in late 2024; current data shows zero-to-negative reach impact. 0–3 cosmetic at most, never a strategy.
- **Keyword-stuffed headlines** — semantic ranking reads meaning, not density; stuffing reads as spam to humans and adds nothing for the model.
- **Profile as resume** — third-person bios, duty lists, "results-driven professional" boilerplate. The profile sells the one action or it does nothing.
- **Multiple CTAs** — newsletter + course + podcast + book a call, all at once. One action. The Featured free/paid pair covers both readiness levels without splitting the message.
