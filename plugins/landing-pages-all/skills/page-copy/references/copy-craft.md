# Copy Craft — headlines, sections, CTAs, banned phrases

The line-level rules. The anatomy file says what sections exist; this file says how the sentences inside them get written.

## The master equation

Every edit must do one of three things: **raise desire, cut labor, or cut confusion** (Shapiro's Conversion = Desire − Labor − Confusion). An edit that does none of these is decoration — revert it.

## Headlines

**The three lenses (Dry):**

- **A. Explain** — state what the offer is and does, plainly. Earns the hero only when the offer is genuinely distinct; "we do the thing everyone does, said plainly" is a wasted hero.
- **B. Objection flip** — most offers aren't unique, so lead with the reader's biggest objection turned into a claim. The objection comes from the VOC table, not from imagination.
- **C. Own the niche** — the conviction one-liner: this is THE answer for exactly this reader. Works when the targeting is narrow and the operator can back the swagger.

**The lint (every candidate passes all of these or dies):**

1. **Falsifiable** — could the claim be proven wrong? A number, a named mechanism, a timeframe qualifies; "world-class" and "the best way to" don't. This is the single strongest excellent-vs-average separator in current practice (Dry's third law: concrete, visual, falsifiable).
2. **Stranger-parseable in 5 seconds** — would someone with no context glance at it and correctly grunt back what's for sale? (The caveman test.)
3. **Ultra-specific** — the 4U lint (useful, urgent, unique, ultra-specific; hit at least 3) is still a usable filter; ultra-specific is the non-negotiable one.
4. **Customer language** — prefer constructions real customers used. The published case work on language/market fit shows headline language alone moving signup conversion from ~3% to ~30% — it is the highest-leverage line on the page.
5. **Zero banned phrases** (list below).

**Subhead rules (Shapiro):** maximum 2 sentences. Sentence 1 = what it is (category a stranger recognizes). Sentence 2 = the mechanism that makes the headline's claim believable. Title carries the value; subhead carries the how.

## Section copy

- **Feature/component blocks:** short non-vague header (a verb-y capability, not a label) + a tight paragraph that also answers that feature's own objection + a real-visual direction. Every block ties back to the hero promise — the running narrative.
- **Pain sections:** the reader's words, present tense, specific scenes — not adjectives about pain. "You rewrote the proposal at 11pm again" beats "juggling endless tasks."
- **Testimonial shape:** concrete result + number + timeframe + name (and face when building the page). Bold the takeaway line. Place each one beside the claim it validates; one near every CTA. A testimonial that validates nothing specific is filler.
- **Founder's note:** four beats — put yourself in their shoes → name their problem → take ownership of it → show the happy ending. First-person singular. This is where the voice profile matters most; quirks survive editing here on purpose.
- **Numbers:** every number on the page is real and, where it could move (subscriber counts, client counts), current per the operator. No rounding up, no "thousands" when it's hundreds.

## CTAs

- **Call-to-value over call-to-action:** the button completes the promise. Test: does it complete "**I want to ___**" in the reader's mouth? "Get my heatmap" passes; "Submit" and "Learn more" fail. First person ("my", "me") measurably outperforms second person in published button tests.
- **Objection microcopy at the button:** the one fear closest to the click, answered in small text — "Free for 14 days · cancel anytime", "Pay once, keep forever", "3 spots left this month" (only when literally true).
- **Distribution on long pages:** 3–4 CTAs — top (for the already-convinced), mid-page after proof, bottom after FAQ/guarantee. Single-CTA dogma on a long page is dated advice; so is treating the fold as everything (the fold's only job is earning the scroll).
- **Repeat CTAs restate the argument:** one line of why, built from claims already proven above — never a bare button floating in whitespace.
- **Opt-ins:** email field + button together; collect everything else after, not on the page.

## The banned-phrase list (enforced, not advisory)

These are the averaging words — they fit every company, so they sell none. They are also the recognized AI-slop tells of 2025–26, which makes them double poison.

**Banned claims:** all-in-one · seamless(ly) · effortless(ly) · revolutionary / game-changing · supercharge · unlock / unleash your potential · next-level · cutting-edge · world-class · best-in-class · state-of-the-art · "takes X to the next level" · "like never before" · faster/better/easier *unqualified* (with a real number and baseline, comparisons are fine — that's the falsifiable version)

**Banned constructions:** "Build the future of ___" · "Your all-in-one platform for ___" · "Scale without limits" · vague aspirational heroes that name no product · `[adjective] + [noun] + "that helps you" + [verb]` as the mold for every feature block · "It's not just X, it's Y" · empty empowerment ("empower", "elevate", "transform the way you...")

**The replacement rule:** every banned phrase gets replaced by the *specific* it was hiding. "Seamless integration" → name the two tools and what passes between them. "Save time" → how much, on what task. If no specific exists, the claim was empty — cut it, don't re-dress it.

## Where the rules flex

- The voice profile's evidenced patterns override generic style guidance — if the operator genuinely talks in short punchy fragments or pet phrases, the page keeps them. The banned-claims list above does not flex; it's about claim emptiness, not style.
- Awareness stage can compress sections, reorder nothing.
- Humor, edge, and opinion are allowed and usually good — relentless neutral positivity is itself a slop tell. The operator's page should sound like one person who believes something.
