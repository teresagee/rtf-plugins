# The Quality Pass — run before the operator sees a draft

Five tests plus a scan. Run them in order; record pass/fail and what changed in `brief.md`. A draft that fails any of the first three is not ready to show.

## 1. The five-second test (caveman test)

Read only the headline, subhead, and button. Would a stranger with zero context correctly say what's for sale and for whom? If the answer needs the rest of the page, the hero failed. (This is the failure behind most low-converting pages: a headline a stranger can't parse in five seconds.)

Cheap real-world version to offer the operator: show the hero to 3–4 actual humans for five seconds, then ask "what did it say? what would it do for you?" — four people per headline, iterate. An afternoon of this is worth more than weeks of opinion.

## 2. The in-person sell test

For every section ask: "If I were selling this face to face, would saying this help?" Anything that would sound absurd spoken to a real prospect — slogans, mission-statement filler, third-person self-praise — gets cut. (Dry's final filter.)

## 3. Falsifiability density

Walk the page and count claims. Each one is either **falsifiable** (a number, a named mechanism, a timeframe, a checkable fact) or **vibes**. Excellent pages run dense on falsifiable claims; average pages are vibes all the way down. Any claim that is falsifiable but *unproven on the page* needs its receipt adjacent — or a `[PROOF GAP]` flag, never a fabricated receipt.

## 4. The felt-pain check

Does the page ever make the problem acute, in the reader's own words — or does it only mention that a problem exists? The most common paid-teardown finding is a page whose visitor *has* the pain but never *feels* it on the page. One concrete scene beats three adjectives.

Companion check — **whose page is this?** Count sentences about the reader vs sentences about the brand. Brand-centric pages ("we believe", "our journey") are the second classic failure.

## 5. The halve-the-page cut

"If this page had to be half as long, what goes?" Identify the weakest half-candidates. Anything that wouldn't survive the halving is probably labor and confusion already — cut or compress now. (Shapiro's brevity question.) Then the inverse: "what triggers skepticism?" — find the disbelief points and reinforce them with proof or soften the claim to what's provable.

## 6. The AI-slop scan

Run last, against the final text. The full blocklist lives in the Voice plugin's ai-tells reference and in the operator's installed voice profile; for page copy these are the tells that matter most:

- **Averaging copy:** any sentence that would be true of every company in the niche ("exceptional solutions that drive meaningful results"). Replace with the specific or cut.
- **The feature-block mold:** every block shaped `[adjective] + [noun] + "that helps you" + [verb]`. Vary structure; lead blocks with verbs and capabilities.
- **Banned phrases** from copy-craft.md — zero tolerance in the deliverable.
- **Committee voice:** perfect grammar, zero opinion, both-sides hedging, relentless positivity. A page by one person who believes something reads human; sand in an opinion, a quirk, a first-person aside (the founder's note especially).
- **Structural tells:** "It's not just X, it's Y" · staccato negation triplets · rule-of-three on autopilot · uniform paragraph rhythm · bullets where an argument should be prose.
- **Read-aloud test:** read the hero and founder's note aloud. Anything you'd never say to a person gets rewritten in words you would.

Calibration notes: clusters convict, single hits don't — one em dash is a punctuation mark, four per paragraph is a tell. And the voice profile's evidenced quirks always override the blocklist: if the operator genuinely uses a flagged word (proven by their samples), it stays. Vendor claims that slop-styled pages convert dramatically worse exist but are unverified — the honest case for this scan is simpler: readers now recognize these patterns and discount the page on sight.
