# Page Anatomies — archetype selection and section orders

Four archetypes cover what operators actually ship. Pick by what the page sells and how aware the reader arrives; then follow the section order. These compose the practitioner frameworks that still hold up as of mid-2026 — Harry Dry's 10-step anatomy, Julian Shapiro's 7-element skeleton, Fletch PMM's homepage order, and the long-form offer-page consensus for courses/coaching/services.

## Choosing the archetype

| The page sells... | Reader arrives... | Archetype |
|---|---|---|
| A course, program, coaching offer, paid community | problem- or solution-aware | **Offer page** (long-form) |
| Productized or done-for-you service | solution-aware, comparison-shopping | **Service page** |
| A software product / the business itself (B2B) | mixed; increasingly pre-qualified by AI summaries | **Homepage** (Fletch order) |
| A newsletter, lead magnet, waitlist | any; the ask is only an email | **Opt-in page** (minimal) |

**Awareness adjusts length, not order:** less aware = longer top (start at the pain); more aware = compress or skip the problem section and get to the offer. As of 2026, visitors referred by AI assistants have typically read a summary of the offer first — they arrive solution/product-aware and convert better, and a homepage now does landing-page duty for them (most AI referrals land on homepages, not deep pages). Don't write the whole page for the coldest visitor.

**Framework selection keys off awareness, not format** (the 2025–26 consensus on the classic formulas): problem-aware sections run PAS (problem–agitate–solution); transformation offers frame BAB (before–after–bridge); warm/informed sections use feature–proof patterns (FAB/4Ps energy). Pages stack frameworks per-section — no single formula runs top to bottom.

## 1. Offer page (long-form — courses, programs, coaching)

The solo-operator workhorse. Transformation framing (BAB) over capability framing.

1. **Hero** — headline (transformation, falsifiable), subhead (what it is + the mechanism), CTA, one line of claim-validating proof.
2. **Pain** — PAS: name the reader's current struggle in their own words and make it felt. Most pages skip the "felt" part — the visitor has the problem but the page never makes it acute (the most common finding from paid page teardowns).
3. **Transformation** — the after-state, concrete: what their week/work/numbers look like on the other side. BAB bridge into the offer.
4. **Program breakdown** — what's actually inside: modules, sessions, deliverables. Specific enough to be falsifiable; each component tied to the promised transformation.
5. **Proof** — testimonials placed *next to the claims they validate*, not in one wall. Best shape: concrete result + number + timeframe + name/face. Bold the takeaway line for skimmers.
6. **The offer stack** — everything included, with the price stated plainly. If a value-contrast is used, every number in it must be real (actual prices the components sell for) — never theater math.
7. **Guarantee / risk reversal** — whatever is actually offered. Don't invent one; ask the operator.
8. **FAQ** — leftover objections as plain Q&A (see AEO note below).
9. **Founder's note** — near-mandatory for solo operators, not optional. Narrative arc: their shoes → their problem → "I built this because" → the happy ending. The most voice-dependent section on the page.
10. **Final CTA** — restates the argument; objection microcopy at the button.

3–4 CTAs total, distributed: after hero, after proof, after FAQ/guarantee.

## 2. Service page (productized / done-for-you)

Dry's 10-step anatomy is the spine; the twist is **show the work itself**. High-ticket trust moves fastest when the page shows real deliverables instead of describing them (the pattern proven by people who sell teardowns by displaying teardowns).

1. **Hero** — headline, subhead (what + mechanism), CTA + microcopy, proof line.
2. **The work, shown** — real samples, real before/afters, a real deliverable embedded or excerpted. This section outranks any amount of "what we do" prose.
3. **How it works** — the 3-step engagement, concrete: what they send, what happens, what they get, in what timeframe.
4. **Features & objections** — the 2–3 elements of the service that prove the headline promise, each answering its own objection ("what if it doesn't fit my niche?").
5. **Proof in action** — clients living the result, claims-adjacent testimonials.
6. **FAQ** — pricing-adjacent and process objections.
7. **Founder's note** — the person behind the service; first-person singular.
8. **Final CTA** — restated argument.

## 3. Homepage (B2B / SaaS-style — Fletch order)

The homepage is positioning made visible. It must answer five questions fast: what does it do / how does it work / what does it look like / when would I use it / how does it help me make progress.

1. **Hero** — written for the *champion* (closest to the pain, not the budget holder): what the product lets them do, in an existing category they recognize. Real product visual — as of 2025–26 an embedded interactive demo is the SaaS norm over static screenshots; flag that as the visual direction.
2. **Problem section** — empathy plus a qualifier that *repels bad-fit visitors* (a page afraid to disqualify converts nobody).
3. **Solution intro** — category + primary capability + main benefit, in three lines a stranger can repeat back.
4. **Three value-prop sections** — each one capability (what the user can do) + the feature that delivers it + the benefit, with a product-in-action visual. Lead with capability: informed buyers shop what it *does*, not benefit poetry.
5. **Bottom half, fitted to the business** — case studies, persona/industry routing ("for coaches / for agencies" — route, never average), ROI numbers that are real, testimonials.
6. **FAQ + final CTA.**

CTA split for SaaS: low-friction path (trial/start) and trust path (demo/call) — both present, visually ranked by which the business actually wants.

## 4. Opt-in page (newsletter, lead magnet, waitlist)

Minimal by design — the ask is one email, so the page earns one decision.

1. **Headline** — the specific value of subscribing/downloading, falsifiable ("Every Tuesday: one teardown of X" beats "Insights to your inbox").
2. **3 bullets or one short paragraph** — what's inside, concretely.
3. **Proof line** — real subscriber count *only if real and worth saying*, or one named-reader quote. No count? A specific sample issue link beats a vague claim.
4. **Email field + CTA** — first-person button, objection microcopy ("free · unsubscribe anytime" — only claims that are true).
5. **Optional: one sample** — show a real issue/extract of the lead magnet.

Collect the email and nothing else; everything else can be asked later.

## The AEO layer (applies to every archetype)

A growing share of buyers arrive pre-sold by an AI assistant that read the page first — so the page must be parseable by machines as well as people. This is Answer Engine Optimization (AEO); never frame it as SEO.

- **Unambiguous entity statement:** somewhere above the fold the page says plainly what the product/offer *is* and *for whom* — no riddle heroes.
- **Answer-first FAQ:** real questions phrased the way buyers ask them, each with a direct first-sentence answer.
- **Comparison honesty:** if buyers compare this offer against a named alternative, a plain "how this differs from X" passage helps both the human and the machine — kept factual, no trash-talk.
- These overlap almost perfectly with good conversion copy; AEO is a reason to do the anatomy properly, not extra work.

## Calibration honesty

When the operator asks "what conversion rate should I expect": the latest major verifiable dataset (Unbounce's 41k-page benchmark report, 2024 — still the reference as of mid-2026) puts the median around 6.6% across industries, with SaaS nearer 4% and high-intent categories into the teens. Quote it as a dated benchmark, not a promise — and never predict a specific rate for their page.
