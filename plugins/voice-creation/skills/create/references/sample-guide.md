# Sample Guide

What counts as source material, what works best per subject type, and the one rule that protects the whole profile.

---

## The contamination rule (read this first)

**Samples must be raw — written or spoken by the actual person, before any AI touched them.**

Anything composed in a chat with Claude (or any other AI), anything an AI "cleaned up," anything generated from an outline — contaminated. A profile built on AI-assisted text learns to sound like an AI's idea of the subject, and every future piece inherits that. The profile can't be better than its samples.

- Pre-AI material is gold: old newsletters, old emails, old posts, anything from before the subject started using AI tools.
- Spoken material is nearly impossible to contaminate: transcripts of the subject talking are among the best samples available.
- When a sample's origin is unclear, ask when and how it was written. If the honest answer is "Claude helped," leave it out.

## Best samples per subject

### Self (the operator's own voice)

- Newsletters, blog posts, articles they drafted themselves
- Transcripts of them talking — videos, podcast appearances, talks, voice memos transcribed
- Personal notes and journal entries — no audience filter, very high signal
- Sent messages — Slack/Discord/email where they explained something or argued a point
- Social posts they actually wrote (not scheduled AI output)

The more varied the formats, the better the profile generalizes.

### Client (a specific person)

- Their published material — newsletters, columns, articles
- Transcripts of them talking — interviews, podcasts, internal videos (often MORE authentic than their polished writing; prioritize these for ghostwriting)
- Their social presence — how the person actually posts, not the corporate boilerplate
- Anything they've flagged as "this sounds like me" or "this doesn't"

### Brand (a house voice)

- The brand's best-performing published pieces
- The style guide or brand book, if one exists (treat as stated intent — the published material is the evidence)
- Customer-facing writing: onboarding emails, product pages, support macros
- Whatever the brand's own team points to as the reference pieces

## What this skill does NOT do

- **Transcribe audio.** Bring text. If the material is video or audio, transcribe it first (if a transcription skill is installed in this workspace, use that), then paste or point to the transcript.
- **Scrape behind paywalls or logins.** Public URLs can be fetched; everything else the operator pastes.
- **Generate samples.** The voice has to come from somewhere real. If there's no material, there's no profile yet — gather material first.

## Volume guidance

- **Minimum:** 3 substantial pieces (over 200 words each). Below this, the profile is marked low-confidence.
- **Sweet spot:** 5–8 substantial pieces across at least 2 formats.
- **Too few:** the profile goes vague and AI defaults leak into output.
- **Too many:** diminishing returns past ~10. Variety beats volume.

## Accepted input formats

- **Pasted text** — the default. Paste it raw, typos and all.
- **File paths** — files already in the workspace; they're read in place and referenced by path, not copied.
- **Public URLs** — fetched when possible; pasted as fallback.
- **Mixed** — fine, and often best. Label anything that isn't the subject's own material (e.g. a reference piece) so analysis can weight it correctly.
