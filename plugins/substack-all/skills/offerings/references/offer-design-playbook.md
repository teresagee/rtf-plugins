# Offer design playbook — what paid Substacks actually sell (state of play, mid-2026)

Provenance, stated once: the perk-prevalence percentages come from one analyst's quantified review of hundreds of top-earning Substacks (the only public perk data with percentages found — single source, methodology theirs); the free/paid mix doctrine and reader-motive themes from Substack's own published guidance; tier-craft moves from practitioners who audit conversion surfaces professionally (counts of audits self-reported). Treat percentages as strong directional data, not gospel.

## What top earners actually put in paid tiers (perk-type prevalence)

From the quantified review of top-earning publications:

| Perk type | Share of top earners offering it |
|---|---|
| Content (extra/full posts, archive) | 97% |
| Community (chat, threads, private space) | 71% |
| Access (to the writer: Q&A, DMs, office hours) | 31% |
| Intrinsic reward ("support my work") | 26% |
| Live events (calls, workshops, streams) | 19% |
| Digital products (templates, courses, tools) | 16% |
| Physical goods | 13% |
| Discounts on other products | 9% |
| Services | 6% |
| Gift subscriptions | 2% |
| Social capital (recognition, credits) | 2% |

**The design reading:** content is table stakes — it appears almost everywhere, so it differentiates nothing alone. Winners **stack 2–3 perk types** (content + community + access is the classic stack). The 2026 shift across credible trend analyses: the paid tier reads as a **membership** — templates, mini-courses, workshops, live calls, a chat that's actually alive — because "more articles" is exactly what subscription fatigue is fatigued of.

**The sustainability filter outranks the prevalence table.** A perk the operator resents by month 3 is worse than no perk: it churns paid subscribers *and* burns the operator. Every perk passes: "will you still happily deliver this in month 9, on a bad week?"

## The four free/paid mixes (Substack's own doctrine)

1. **All-free (patronage):** paid unlocks nothing; people pay to support. Honest and low-pressure; earns least; viable for mission-driven writing with a devoted base.
2. **Loosely defined:** paid gets "occasional extras," undefined. Fine as an experiment phase; weak as a standing offer — undefined value converts poorly.
3. **Clearly defined (the growth default):** specific named things live behind the wall — a private community, bonus formats, the archive. What most successful conversions run on.
4. **All-paid:** nearly everything gated. Works mainly in business/finance niches where readers expense the subscription; starves the free growth engine everywhere else.

Supporting datum from Substack's published data: publications that paywall at least some content earn on the order of **5x** those that paywall nothing. Free is the engine, but a wall somewhere is what monetizes it.

**What free must do in the system:** Notes fills the free list (that's the notes skill's lane); free content proves the paid promise. If free is thin, the paid tier has no evidence — design free as the demonstration, not the leftovers.

## Why readers actually pay (the eight motive themes)

From Substack's published reader research — the raw material for "why pay" copy, always translated into the ICP's own language:

1. Supporting the mission · 2. Fandom of the writer · 3. Ethos of paying creators · 4. The work's direct value to them · 5. Intimate access · 6. Community · 7. Education · 8. Backing a cause

A strong "why pay" block speaks 2–3 of these — whichever the ICP profile actually supports — not all eight.

## Founding tier — superfan capital, not a bigger paid

Researched norms among publications that do it well:

- Priced as patronage-plus: typically **$150–250+/yr** (observed range among researched publications, not a rule — anchor against the paid annual price, usually 2–4x).
- **2–3 exclusive perks + genuine VIP recognition** — a yearly 1:1 call, name in the credits, first access, a founding-member channel. Distinct from paid, not paid-with-a-tip.
- The honest fallback: if no real exclusive perk exists, frame founding as **intrinsic support with recognition** — common (26% of top earners offer intrinsic-reward framing) and better than inventing perks that won't survive month 3.

## Writing the tier copy (craft rules)

- **Transformations, not features.** "2 emails per week" is a feature; the transformation is what those emails *do* for the reader — name the outcome, in the ICP's words, and make sure the deliverable can cash the promise. Shape: *[what they get] so [what changes for them]*.
- **Bundle-frame the stack.** One stacked list per tier reading as "look how much this holds" (the reference high-converting About page in the business niche does exactly this) — not a sparse comparison grid.
- **Free tier gets real benefits copy too.** It sells the upgrade by proving the quality bar.
- **Social proof on the subscribe surface** — count, testimonial, notable subscribers — only if real, exactly as the operator states it.
- **Tier-specific messaging** in the subscribe homepage block (customizable since spring 2026): one short line per tier answering "why this tier, for whom."
- Reframe to keep in mind: the "No thanks" link reads to the visitor as "let me read first" — free content quality is part of the paid funnel, not separate from it.

## Mechanics (where the copy actually goes, mid-2026)

- Tier prices and per-tier **Subscription Benefits** lists are set in the publication dashboard (payments settings); the subscribe page renders tiers side-by-side from those lists.
- Posts target **Everyone / Paid / Free-only / Founding-only** — the targeting menu is the wall; the benefits lists are the promise. They must match.
- The **subscribe homepage block** (shipped spring 2026) takes colors, logo, and tier-specific messaging.
- **Substack Boost** is on by default with payments enabled: it auto-surfaces discounts at high-intent moments, including a save offer at cancellation. Operators should know it exists before designing their own discounts — and that the winback email sequence (welcome-sequence skill's lane) should coordinate with it, not duplicate it.

## Anti-patterns (seen constantly in weak offers)

- Paid tier = "more of the same, slightly more often"
- Founding tier = paid tier with a bigger number and no story
- Perks copied from a bigger publication's page without the capacity behind them
- Benefit lists written as the operator's chores ("I will send...") instead of the reader's outcomes
- A paid promise the free content gives no evidence for
