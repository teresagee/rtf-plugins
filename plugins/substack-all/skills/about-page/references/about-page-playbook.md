# About page playbook — structures, formulas, benchmarks (state of play, mid-2026)

Provenance, stated once: the structural checklist comes from Substack's own published guidance; the reader-sequence and layering framework from a practitioner's analysis of 100 About pages; the one-liner formula and byline frames from practitioners who publish their methods openly. Conversion benchmarks are practitioner-reported, not audited. Where only one source backs a claim, it's marked.

## How a visitor actually reads this page

A practitioner's 100-page analysis found readers run a fixed mental sequence in under ~60 seconds: **credibility check → value evaluation → community belonging.** Three questions, in order: *can I trust this person? what exactly do I get? do people like me belong here?* The page is structured to answer them in that order — and everything load-bearing lands in the **first 200 words**, because most visitors never reach word 201.

### The three layers (in the reader's order, not the writer's)

1. **About you** — story and stake, not résumé. The specific moment or perspective that makes this person the one to write this — a job title is not credibility, an origin scene is.
2. **About the publication** — what a subscriber concretely receives: formats, cadence, free vs paid. Concrete nouns ("a teardown every Tuesday") beat abstractions ("regular insights").
3. **About the reader** — the problem this solves for them, and the belonging line: the sentence that makes the right reader feel the room was built for them.

### The four opening moves (any of these; never "Hi, I'm [Name], I'm a [title]")

1. **Bold or counterintuitive claim** — a position the publication actually defends.
2. **Origin moment** — the scene where this publication became necessary.
3. **The problem you solve** — open inside the reader's frustration, in their words.
4. **Community welcome** — "you're one of us" framing, only if a community genuinely exists.

## The official element checklist (Substack's own guidance)

Every About page covers, somewhere:

1. **Publication focus** — topics, post types, the reader benefit, who it's for
2. **Short author bio** — why this person is qualified (delivered as story per Layer 1)
3. **Logistics** — how often it ships, what it costs
4. **Paid subscriber benefits** — what paying actually unlocks
5. **Multiple subscribe buttons** — typically 3 down the page; mark placements `[SUBSCRIBE BUTTON]`, Substack renders them

Optional layers, used only when their condition is true: photos (humanize), manifesto (genuinely held worldview), testimonials/press (real ones only), featured or unlocked posts (best work exists and is linkable), "why pay" explanation (paid tier exists), special offers (one is actually running).

## A reference artifact worth studying (large product/growth newsletter, ~1M+ subscribers)

The best-known high-converting About page in the business niche does five things worth noticing:

- Headline = **social proof + one-sentence promise** ("join [real count] subscribers" + exactly what the advice column delivers and for whom)
- Paid benefits as **one stacked, bundle-framed list** — every issue + interviews + full archive + a private community + a bundle of partner products. The frame is "look how much the bundle holds," not a features table
- **Three subscribe CTAs** spaced down the page
- A **shareable "expense it to your manager" email template** — a B2B move worth copying *if* the operator's ICP can expense subscriptions
- **No pricing on the page itself** — the subscribe flow handles price; the page sells the promise

Study the moves, never the wording.

## The publication one-liner (the highest-leverage sentence on the publication)

It renders on the homepage, recommendation cards, search results, and the welcome page — it works everywhere or nowhere. The test, from Substack's own guidance: the right reader reads it and says **"that's me."**

**The three-element formula** (one practitioner's, clean and reliable):

> **specific audience + the core need it serves + tangible outcome**

The six core needs to pick from: security/freedom · connection/love · status/recognition · access/knowledge · health/longevity · time/convenience.

- Fail: "Thoughts on life, business, and creativity." (no audience, no need, no outcome)
- Pass shape: "Weekly [concrete deliverable] for [specific person] who wants [tangible outcome]."

Always draft 3 candidates against the ICP profile and let the operator pick.

**Discovery note:** practitioner-documented — Substack search indexes publication **titles**, not taglines. If discoverability matters, keywords belong in the title field (a parenthetical subtitle works). Flag once; renaming is the operator's call.

## The profile byline (~1–2 lines under the name, visible all over Notes)

Lead with **who you help + the transformation**; credentials trail or stay out. Frames that work (pick by what the operator's proof supports):

- **3W:** who you are + what you do + who you help
- **Proof + promise:** one real receipt + what the reader gets
- **Transformation:** the before→after the operator produces
- **Reverse positioning:** what this publication is *not* (works in crowded niches)
- **Document, don't preach:** "documenting [the real journey]" — for operators building in public without big receipts yet

Real headshot over logo or illustration: people subscribe to people (consistent practitioner advice).

## Benchmarks and honesty rules

- Visitor→subscriber conversion on the About page: typically **1–3%**, strong pages near **5%** (practitioner-reported). Useful for expectations, never as a promise.
- ~96% of one practitioner's tracked subscribers decided on Notes/profile surfaces before reading a full post (single source, self-reported) — the reason the About page, byline, and one-liner are maintained as first-class surfaces.
- **Social proof only if real**: counts, testimonials, press — exactly as the operator states them or not at all. A page with zero proof points and total honesty outperforms one fake number the moment a reader checks.
- **The page must agree with the system**: frequency and paid benefits match `business.md` and `workspace/substack/offer.md`. A contradiction between surfaces is a conversion leak and a trust leak at once.
