# Note formats — the rotation taxonomy

Synthesized from the practitioners publishing tracked subscriber-source data as of mid-2026 (several independent systems converge hard), cross-checked against a 2026 scrape of 243,000+ notes. Use this as the working taxonomy: rotate through it, let the operator's tracker promote the formats that convert for *them*.

## The register (non-negotiable, every format)

- **Talk, don't lecture.** A note reads like a message to one person, not a TED talk. Short sentences. One idea.
- **50–250 words, 1–5 paragraphs, single-sentence lines, white space.** Scannable like a billboard, not dense like a textbook.
- **The 2-second test:** the point must be understood — and feel relatable — within two seconds of scroll-stopping. If the reader has to work, the feed has already moved.
- An occasional image or short video lifts a note; Notes supports video and embeds natively.

## The anatomy (every note, every format)

1. **Hook — line 1.** Specific over clever; a real number where one truthfully exists ("specific hook with a number" is the convergent line-1 rule across sources). It earns the next line, nothing more.
2. **Body — 2–5 lines.** The story, insight, or fix. One clear point, not five vague ones.
3. **CTA — the last line.** A genuine question that invites conversation. **Never "subscribe."** Comments outweigh likes in distribution, and conversation is what converts followers into subscribers over time.

The convergent conversion formula underneath: **hook → story or insight → proof.** People don't subscribe because a note was quotable; they subscribe because it showed them something possible and proved it was real. Proof means a *real* specific — a number, a result, a named situation from the operator's actual world. No real proof available → use a format below that doesn't require one.

## The eight formats

### 1. Quick fix
Solve one real problem the ICP has, completely, in under 60 seconds of reading. Converts because the reader extrapolates: if the free stuff is this useful, the newsletter must be worth the inbox.
- Needs: one genuinely useful, specific tactic from the operator's expertise.
- Skeleton: problem named in their words → the fix, step-shaped if possible → one line on the result it produces → "what's your version of this?"
- Failure mode: generic advice anyone could give. If it would survive on a motivational account, cut it.

### 2. Story with receipt
A real personal moment anchored to a specific true number. The archetype across tracked accounts: "[small action] led to [specific measurable result]" told as a moment, not a brag.
- Needs: a real number from the operator (revenue, subscribers, hours, a count). **Skill must source it, never invent it.**
- Skeleton: the moment → what happened, with the number → what it taught → question.
- Failure mode: fabricated or rounded-up receipts. One invented number poisons the whole account's credibility.

### 3. Generosity
Promote other creators with zero angle — name them, say specifically why they're worth reading, ask nothing. In the richest public dataset, a pure generosity note was the **highest single-note subscriber converter the practitioner ever recorded** — generosity converts faster than tactics or vulnerability. It also feeds the overlap machine: their audiences see you.
- Needs: creators the operator genuinely reads (ask; never recommend someone the operator doesn't actually rate).
- Skeleton: "people you should be reading" framing → 2–5 names with one specific reason each → "who am I missing?"
- Failure mode: strategic flattery aimed at whales. Readers smell it; recommend mid-tier writers the operator actually values.

### 4. Contrarian take
Challenge one accepted assumption in the niche — with a reason, not just an edge.
- Needs: a position the operator actually holds (check user.md and business.md; never assign them an opinion).
- Skeleton: the common advice, stated fairly → why it breaks, concretely → what to do instead → "am I wrong?"
- Failure mode: contrarianism as a costume. Manufactured hot takes earn likes from strangers and subscribers from no one.

### 5. Confession
A real failure, fear, or struggle — no resolution required. Shared-struggle notes are among the strongest subscriber-converters in practitioner analyses: "I almost quit X. Not because [surface reason]. Because [real reason]." → the doubt → the turning point or just the honest state → the lesson if there is one.
- Needs: something true and unresolved-enough from the operator. Ask; don't dramatize their bio.
- Failure mode: vulnerability theater — a humblebrag wearing a confession's clothes.

### 6. Behind the scenes
Open the books: a milestone, a revenue or subscriber breakdown, what's working and what isn't. Transparency builds the trust that paid tiers are later bought on.
- Needs: real metrics the operator is willing to publish. Confirm willingness before drafting — this format is public by definition.
- Failure mode: vague "growth has been great lately" — the entire value is the specific number.

### 7. Conversation starter
A genuine question the ICP wants to answer, engineered for comments — because comments outrank likes in distribution and every reply is a relationship.
- Needs: a question with real stakes for the audience (from ICP.md's frustrations/aspirations), one the operator actually wants answers to.
- Skeleton: light setup (1–2 lines, an opinion helps) → the question → optionally "I'll go first:" with the operator's own answer.
- Failure mode: engagement-bait questions with no stakes ("coffee or tea?"). The algorithm may not punish it; the audience does.

### 8. Permission note
Encouragement with spine, for the reader who thinks they're too late or too slow: timing fear → honest social proof → validation. Includes the consistency variant: "spent [real time] on [hard thing]. It didn't work. Tomorrow I'll try again." Builds the emotional bond that converts quietly.
- Needs: honesty about the operator's own timeline (from user.md) — permission given from real experience, not from above.
- Failure mode: AI-flavored motivational paste. If it could be a quote card, delete it.

## Daily slots (the tested shape of a 3-note day)

- **Morning — community:** an invitation or identity note ("if [condition], you're [identity] — say hi" energy: formats 3, 7, 8). Opens conversation for the day.
- **Midday — teach:** one actionable thing (formats 1, 2, 4, 6). The proof-carrier of the day.
- **Evening — reflect:** the honest one (formats 5, 8). Reflection converts at night, per tracked windows.

## The 80/20 rule

Of every 10 notes, at least 8 are pure value or generosity with no ask; at most 2 softly *tease* the newsletter or paid tier (name what a subscriber-only piece solves — don't sell it, don't link it). Conversational notes outperform promotional ones roughly 3–5x on early velocity in practitioner tests, and promotional drift (>2 promo notes in your last 10) measurably drags reach.

## Instant kills (never produce these)

- "Follow me for more" / "subscribe for more" endings
- A newsletter headline + link reposted as a note
- AI-generated motivational quotes and quote-card profundity
- Walls of text — no white space, multi-clause sentences stacked
- Deep-sounding aphorisms optimized for likes: the 243K-note scrape's headline finding is that likability and conversion are different axes — 40% of the most-liked notes converted zero subscribers
