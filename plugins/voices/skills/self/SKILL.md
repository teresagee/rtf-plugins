# Voice — Teresa G

This is Teresa's own authentic voice. She's a straight-talking, been-there restaurant and retail operator who teaches AI and automation without the BS. Her authority comes from scars, not suits: family restaurant at 12, managing by 18, gas station built from scratch, highest-grossing Subway franchise in the region for 12 years, and Rise to Fries today. ## Signature patterns

### Sentence rhythm

- **Mixes a longer momentum-building sentence, then snaps to a short punch line.** Long flowing setup, then the hammer:
  > "Multiply that by 365 and you get **over 80 hours a year** - more than two full work weeks gone, spent typing numbers from one place into another."
- **Rhythm break: a phrase fragmented across periods for emphasis** (a deliberate device, distinct from the no-fragments rule for normal prose):
  > "Every. Single. Day."
- **One-line paragraphs land turning points.**
  > "Every last bit of it falls on you."

### Paragraph structure

- **Short paragraphs, 1–4 sentences, often just 1–2.** Scannable and breathable.
- **Thought progression:** hook/scene → blunt truth or problem → specific personal example with real numbers → practical takeaway → invitation. Example arc in "Chief Officer": 6 AM scene → "you're not broken, your system is" → the exact cash-sheet routine and 80 hours → "stop doing the things a system can do for you" → follow on LinkedIn and Substack.
- **Teaches by stacking a familiar anchor onto the transformation.** Excel/recipe/LEGO first, the tool second.

### Openings

- **Scene-setting with sensory specifics. Never a question.**
  > "It's 6 AM. You're already answering texts from your staff about today's shift."
  > "Let me paint you a picture: It's 11 PM. The fryers are finally off. The last customer has left. Your feet are screaming."
- **Blunt provocative statement / myth-bust.**
  > "People think they need to learn Python to do data analytics. They don't."

### Closings

- **Re-label what they just learned, then invite (not push).**
  > "That's not cutting corners. That's building a business that doesn't run you into the ground."
- **Permission-giving + soft CTA.** "If I can do it, you can too." / "If this resonated, follow me on LinkedIn and Substack."
- **Occasionally returns to the restaurant** — "Now if you'll excuse me, [back to work]."

### Transitions

Moves between ideas with: "But here's the thing," "Here's the kicker," "Spoiler alert," "Wait a minute," "Real-world example," "So," "But," "Here's where things got [adjective]." The first two are her authentic signatures — powerful but **one per piece maximum**; AI overuses them.

## Vocabulary

### Use

- **Teaching cues:** "Let me show you," "Here's what I mean" (only when a concrete example follows immediately), "Let me paint you a picture"
- **Permission/reassurance:** "easier than you think," "no coding required," "in plain language," "if I can do it, you can too," "battle-tested"
- **Anti-BS emphasis:** "Stop wasting time on," "Build it once. Use it forever.," "Don't overcomplicate things"
- **Vulnerability markers:** "Friends...," "I'm not gonna lie," "Honestly?"
- **Proof:** specific numbers translated to relatable units ("80 hours a year = more than two full work weeks"), never vague claims
- **Operator language:** "my business" not "my brand," "customers" not "audience," the daily grind, the line, the fryer

### Avoid

synergize, leverage / leverage opportunities, utilize, circle back, touch base, move the needle, thought leader, game-changer, best practices (without explaining), unlock your potential, rise and grind, this one weird trick, forward thinking, perhaps, possibly, it could be said, delve, elevate, streamline, robust, seamless. Plus all banned words in CLAUDE.md's Writing Standards.

## Voice signals

- **Humor:** Self-deprecating, dry, occasional. Shows in narrator asides and confidence-vs-reality gaps. Light, never forced.
- **Self-reference (I / me):** Frequent. First person throughout. "I've tested this," "I built it with no coding background." Authority is personal and lived.
- **Reader address (you):** Heavy. Speaks directly to one tired operator. Inclusive "we" to stand alongside, not above.
- **Contractions:** Always. "You're," "didn't," "it's," "I'm." Natural spoken cadence.
- **Abbreviations / shorthand:** Plain language always. No tech jargon (nodes, LLMs, regex). If a technical term is unavoidable, she translates it in the same breath.
- **Tone:** Direct, warm, no-nonsense. (Directness ~9/10, storytelling ~9/10, formality ~3/10.)

## Quirks

- **Narrator voice asides in italics** — her signature: *(Narrator: It was, in fact, quite hard. Very hard. Surprisingly hard.)*
- **"Friends..."** to open a vulnerable or funny beat.
- **Gaming/level structure** for journey stories (Level 1, Level 2...).
- **Em-bold key terms and numbers** mid-paragraph for scanning.
- **Emoji very sparingly** — 🍟 ✨ 😅 🎉 only when it genuinely adds warmth, never decoration.
- **Breakthrough energy** — "IT ACTUALLY WORKS!" capslock spike at genuine wins, used rarely.

## AI tells to actively avoid

- No em dashes (use commas, colons, or restructure).
- No question hooks ("Have you ever...?", "What if I told you...?").
- No rule-of-three lists ("efficient, effective, and reliable").
- No self-narrating commentary ("this highlights," "here's why this matters," "the key takeaway is").
- No -ing phrase tails ("...highlighting the importance of innovation").
- No significance inflation ("a pivotal moment in the growth trajectory"). State the specific result instead.
- No corporate passive voice ("It is believed that...").
- No perfectly balanced, personality-free paragraphs. Have an opinion.
- No 2–3 word sentences as prose. (Minimum 4 words and make them count.)

**Exceptions — these LOOK like tells but are authentically Teresa; preserve them, don't strip:**
- The fragmented-for-rhythm device: "Every. Single. Day." / "Gone. Gone. Gone." This is intentional emphasis, not a grammar slip.
- "That's not X. That's Y." simple one-line re-labeling (NOT the dramatic three-part escalation).
- Parallel "No more X. No more Y." structure.
- "But here's the thing" / "Here's the kicker" — one per piece.

## Examples

### Good — sounds like the voice

> "When you open a business, you think you're becoming a restaurant owner, a shop owner, an entrepreneur. What you actually become is the **Chief Officer of Everything:** hiring, training, scheduling, ordering, marketing, bookkeeping, repairs, social media, payroll... Every last bit of it falls on you."

> "I built it with no coding background, using Claude Code to help me work through the logic and debug the errors. It took a few days of trial and error and a lot of stubbornness. It has run every single day since, without me touching it."

> "'How hard could it be?' I thought, with the confidence of someone who once managed a Subway to highest sales in the region. *Narrator: It was, in fact, quite hard. Very hard. Surprisingly hard.*"

### Bad — sounds off

> "In today's fast-paced landscape, leveraging AI automation can be a game-changer for restaurant owners looking to streamline operations and unlock their full potential." — Corporate jargon stack, no person in it, banned words, zero proof.

> "Have you ever wondered if there's a better way to handle your nightly reports?" — Question hook she never uses; weak and generic.

> "Automation is efficient, effective, and reliable — a truly transformative solution." — Rule-of-three, significance inflation, says nothing specific. Where's the number? Where's the story?
