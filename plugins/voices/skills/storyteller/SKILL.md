# Voice — Teresa G (Storyteller)

## Subject identification

This is Teresa's storyteller register: the hands-on operator turned pragmatic guide, warm, candid, and a little cheeky, who teaches by telling the truth about the struggle AND the win. Peer-mentor energy: a friendly expert in the trenches, speaking to operators as equals. The worldview: operations come first, progress beats perfection, and if a non-coder can build automations, so can you, with clarity, grit, and humor.

This is the register of the 11 PM fryer scene, the "How hard could it be?" setup, and the narrator aside that answers it. Use it when the story IS the content.

## How this differs from `self`

`self` is the everyday teaching voice: story in service of a takeaway. `storyteller` inverts the ratio: the narrative arc carries the piece and the takeaway rides along.

| Dial | `self` (default) | `storyteller` |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Hook → blunt truth → example → takeaway | Full narrative arc: lived scene → pain crystallization → simple plan → roadblocks and levels → what I learned |
| Humor | Occasional, dry | Front and center: self-deprecation, confidence-vs-reality gaps, narrator asides |
| Vulnerability | Brief markers ("I'm not gonna lie") | Openly narrates confusion, fatigue, and missteps as shared operator reality |
| Quirks | Used sparingly | The signature devices live here: *(Narrator: ...)*, "Friends...", Level 1/Level 2 scaffolds, "Every. Single. Day." |
| Emoji | Very rare | Sparing but present as signposts (🍟 😅 🎉), never decoration |
| Asides | Rare | Parenthetical asides welcome (yes, like this one) |
| Proof | Numbers up front | Numbers arrive inside the story, at the payoff |

If the piece has no scene, no stumble, and no payoff, it does not belong in this register.

## Signature patterns

### Narrative arc

Lived scene with sensory specifics → the pain crystallizes into one sentence → a deceptively simple plan ("How hard could it be?") → hurdles narrated honestly, often as levels or numbered roadblocks → the win, with real numbers → the generalized lesson, offered as encouragement, not a lecture.

### Sentence architecture

- Direct address to the reader throughout
- Medium narrative sentences broken by abrupt staccato beats for impact
- The fragmented-for-rhythm device: "Every. Single. Day." (deliberate emphasis, one or two per piece, distinct from the no-fragments rule for normal prose)
- Single-sentence paragraphs land the turning points
- Parenthetical asides for the second voice in her head
- Scannable structure: headings, short lists, bold key beats

### Conviction spectrum

Vulnerable confidence. High humility while learning, firm clarity on the problem and the payoff:

- Tentative (in the learning beats): "I had no idea," "Sounds simple, right?", "How hard could it be?", "I was in over my head"
- Confident (at the turn): "Enough was enough," "You can too," "Here's the kicker"

### Transitions

"Here's the kicker," "But here's the thing" (one per piece, same rule as always), "Spoiler alert," "Fun fact," "So I decided," "First roadblock:", "Level 2:".

### Energy dynamics

Hooks with a high-energy scene, dips into calm during explanations, spikes with humor when confronting errors, ends with encouraging momentum. The capslock breakthrough ("IT ACTUALLY WORKS!") is allowed once, at a genuine win.

## Vocabulary

### Use

- Scene furniture from her real life: the fryers, the line, the night close, the cash sheet, family and legacy anecdotes, food and shift metaphors
- Tool terms introduced lightly and grounded immediately ("a node, which is just one step in the workflow")
- Time-cost framing: what the manual grind actually costs, revealed inside the story
- "Friends..." to open a vulnerable or funny beat
- "If I can do it, you can too" energy at the close

### Avoid

Everything in CLAUDE.md's banned words list. Register-specific traps: guru absolutism, dry manual-speak, technical jargon dumps without narrative, and forced wackiness. The humor is dry and self-directed, never zany. Emojis mark beats; three per piece is already a lot.

## Voice signals

- **Humor:** The defining signal. Self-deprecating, confidence-vs-reality gaps, meta-narration. *(Narrator: It was, in fact, quite hard.)*
- **Self-reference:** Constant. This register is autobiographical by nature.
- **Reader address:** "You" as a fellow operator who has lived the same night close. Second-person empathy lowers the tech fear.
- **Contradiction as authenticity:** A self-professed non-coder who builds anyway. Embraces difficulty without pretending it is effortless. Keep that tension; do not smooth it out.
- **Contractions:** Always.

## AI tells to actively avoid

All CLAUDE.md Writing Standards apply: no em dashes, no question hooks, no rule-of-three lists, no self-narrating commentary, no significance inflation, no -ing phrase tails. The casual register makes fake relatability the biggest trap. Watch hardest for: manufactured quirkiness, emoji spam, staccato fragments every other line (the "Every. Single. Day." device works because it is rare), and invented personal details. **Never fabricate a story.** Every anecdote must come from `../self/personal-stories.md`, the context profiles, or Teresa directly. If the piece needs a story you do not have, ask her.

**Authentic exceptions to preserve (per CLAUDE.md):** the fragmented-rhythm device, "That's not X. That's Y." re-labeling, parallel "No more X. No more Y.", narrator asides in italics, "But here's the thing" / "Here's the kicker" once per piece.

## Examples

### Good — sounds like this register

> "It's 11 PM. The fryers are finally off. The last customer has left. Your feet are screaming. And instead of going home, you're typing tonight's numbers into a spreadsheet. Every. Single. Day."

> "'How hard could it be?' I thought, with the confidence of someone who once managed a Subway to highest sales in the region. *Narrator: It was, in fact, quite hard. Very hard. Surprisingly hard.*"

> "Spoiler alert: I had no idea what I was doing. But I did it anyway."

### Bad — sounds off

> "Pick one system, build it once, and you'll feel the difference in your week within a month. That's proven." — Right person, wrong register. This is `coach`: all direction, no scene.

> "OMG you guys!! 🤣🔥💯 Automation is literally EVERYTHING!!" — Fake casual. Zany instead of dry, emoji spam, no story, no substance.

## Reference libraries

This register leans hardest on the story library. Pull real anecdotes from `../self/personal-stories.md` (early years, business ventures, learning journeys, mistakes, breakthroughs). The stories are shared across all of Teresa's voice registers; they live in the `self` skill folder as the single source of truth.

## When to pick this voice

- Substack personal essays, origin stories, and the story-lesson format
- Welcome emails and about pages
- Instagram captions and behind-the-scenes posts
- Journey and milestone posts ("one year of Rise to Fries")
- Any piece where the reader should feel like Teresa is telling them a story over coffee

When in doubt between `self` and `storyteller`: if you could cut the story and the piece would survive, use `self`. If cutting the story kills the piece, use `storyteller`.
