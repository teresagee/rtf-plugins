# Validation gates + pricing architecture (mid-2026)

The validation sequence below is built on the published playbooks of the practitioners with real product receipts — the Cole/Bush five-step (their numbers, post a self-reported $10M+ in product sales), Jay Clouse's validate-problems-not-products discipline, Katelyn Bourgoin's trigger-event interviewing, and Dan Koe's service-first sequence for operators without an audience. The mechanics are synthesized here in this plugin's own words; the *thresholds* are theirs and are kept because they're the honest part.

---

## Part 1 — The validation gate sequence

The universal consensus across every credible source: **the only real validation is money changing hands, or a demand threshold that historically predicts it.** Everything before that is hypothesis. Sequence: confirm the problem → test demand → harvest language → prove payment → build with buyers.

### Gate 0 — Problem evidence (the skill runs this part)

Before involving the audience at all: a short web scan for people discussing the problem, competing solutions and their prices, and failed attempts in the buyers' own words. Done in Step 3 of the skill, cited, with `[NO EVIDENCE FOUND]` as a legal result. If the operator isn't convinced of viability after seeing the evidence, that's a signal they don't yet understand the audience well enough — a profile problem before a product problem.

### Gate 1 — The waitlist test · threshold: **50+ signups**

One email to the operator's list (or the equivalent post to their primary channel): "I'm thinking about building X — join the waitlist if you'd want it." Single link, no selling, no landing-page production.

- **50+ signups** (the Cole/Bush published viability line) → proceed.
- **A trickle** → don't rationalize. The written re-angle rule applies: change the *problem framing or segment* — not just the title — and re-test once. Two misses → kill, back to the brainstorm doc; the list just saved a build week.
- Adjust honestly for list size: 50 signups from a 500-person list is a stronger signal than 50 from 50,000. Say which case this operator is in.

### Gate 2 — The one-question survey · threshold: **30–50 responses**

Waitlist signup redirects to (or receives) exactly one question: "What's the #1 thing you'd want this to cover about [topic]?"

The harvest is the point: at 30–50 responses you have the audience's **exact language** — and that language becomes the module titles, the landing copy, and the email subject lines. The documented average-creator failure is writing copy in their own jargon; the surveyed phrasing replaces every provisional title in the outline.

### Gate 3 — Interviews · threshold: **~10 conversations**

Ten short (≈20-minute) conversations with waitlisters, traded for early access. The question set:

1. What have you already tried for this?
2. Where exactly did it break down?
3. What outcome would make this worth paying for?
4. What would you need to see to feel confident buying?
5. **The trigger question** (Bourgoin's contribution): what was the *moment* that put you in the market for this — what happened right before you went looking? Triggers are what the launch messaging gets built around; they shift the offer from nice-to-have to need-to-have.

### Gate 4 — The founding cohort · threshold: **3–10 paying (or committed) buyers**

Build the first version *with* a small founding group — discounted founding price or a guarantee, traded explicitly for testimonials and module-by-module feedback. This gate doubles as the launch: by public release the product has proof, and the cohort's words are the sales page.

### The no-list path (swap for Gates 1–2)

An operator without an audience can't clear a waitlist threshold, and pretending otherwise wastes a month. The credible sequence (the Koe service-first pattern): sell a **small consulting or done-for-you engagement on this exact problem** — commonly a 2–4-session pack in the $500–$1,000 range per the practitioners who teach it — to 1–3 buyers found directly. It validates with real money, and the engagement's worksheets, checklists, and process docs *become* the product's modules. The digital product then launches with receipts instead of a waitlist.

### Writing the gates into the plan

Every gate in the scope doc carries three written rules, decided now, not in the moment:

- **Go:** threshold met → next phase unlocks.
- **Re-angle:** threshold missed once → what specifically changes before the single re-test.
- **Kill:** the condition under which this idea returns to the brainstorm doc — stated as a relief, not a failure; the portfolio practitioners kill fast and judge products over multiple bets.

The operator may skip a gate — it's their business — but the skip is recorded in the decision log with their reasoning.

## Part 2 — Pricing architecture

### The two poles (pick one; don't split)

- **Cheap-volume pole:** flat low price (the $150-products-only model proven at large audience scale), no ladder pressure, simplicity as strategy. Requires meaningful audience volume to pay — roughly dozens of sales a day at the proven scale (figures from teardowns of the practitioner's growth era; dated).
- **Ascension-ladder pole:** a deliberate rung structure — free engineered asset → low-ticket entry ($9–$27, classically $27) → core system/course ($97–$300) → flagship ($300–$900) → recurring or high-ticket backend. The published funnel math from the portfolio practitioners (self-reported): a $27 front-end averaging ~$59 per order via order bumps and a ~$39 upsell taken by roughly a third of buyers.
- A third proven shape for subscription-centered stacks: **everything behind one subscription**, with products shipped as drops inside it (the paid-newsletter model with at least one public ~$400k/yr proof, self-reported).

### Placing this product

1. Map the operator's existing offers (`business.md`) onto rungs. The product being scoped either **fills a missing rung**, **feeds an existing one** (free asset → entry, entry → core), or **starts the ladder**. Name which.
2. Choose the band from the taxonomy file, then the number: competitive prices from the evidence scan (cited, dated) set the local range; the rung sets the position in it. Round numbers with a reason beat clever ones without.
3. Set the **founding price** for Gate 4 — meaningfully discounted, explicitly temporary, traded for testimonials. The full price appears alongside it from day one so the discount is legible.
4. Tiered pricing of the same product (e.g. core / plus-community / plus-call tiers) has reported revenue lifts in the 25–40% range — **single-source and promotional; treat as directional**, an experiment for a later relaunch, not a launch requirement.

### Launch shape (decided in the scope, executed later)

- **The founding cohort is the launch.** Gate 4 produces the proof; the public release rides on it.
- **Evergreen after proof:** the documented mid-2026 consensus is hybrid — an always-on funnel (commonly the educational email course: social content → short genuinely-useful email sequence → pitch → non-buyers into a patient nurture loop) layered with 1–2 live pushes a year. Pure launch-dependence is the named burnout pattern; pure evergreen with no live moments leaves money on the table.
- **Relaunch discipline:** quarterly-ish re-releases, judged over multiple iterations. The first launch is data, not a verdict — the work begins when something launches, as the portfolio practitioners put it.
- Standard email launch shape when a live push happens: tease (~2 weeks out) → detail (1 week) → open → mid-window objection email → deadline close; 4–7 emails. The operator sends every one of them; Claude drafts.
